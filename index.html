The Complete Guide to Crepe Myrtle Trees

Understanding Crepe Myrtle Appearance and Characteristics

Crepe myrtle trees are instantly recognizable by their distinctive smooth, mottled bark that peels away in thin sheets to reveal shades of cinnamon, gray, and tan underneath. The bark becomes more pronounced as the tree matures, creating an attractive winter feature when leaves have dropped. During growing season, these deciduous trees produce oval-shaped leaves that emerge bronze or burgundy in spring, transition to dark green through summer, and transform into brilliant yellows, oranges, and reds come autumn.

The most striking feature appears from June through September when crepe myrtles produce large, showy flower clusters called panicles that can measure 6 to 16 inches long depending on the variety. Each individual flower has crinkled, crepe-paper-like petals that give the tree its common name. Mature specimens develop a vase-shaped canopy with multiple trunks, though they can be trained to single-trunk form. The branching structure creates an elegant silhouette that provides year-round visual interest in landscapes across USDA hardiness zones 7 through 9.

Crepe myrtle saplings typically stand 2 to 4 feet tall at purchase and establish quickly when planted correctly. Within three to five years, most varieties reach their bloom-bearing stage and begin showing their mature characteristics. The growth rate averages 3 to 5 feet per year for standard varieties, making them excellent choices for filling landscape gaps relatively quickly. For those seeking immediate impact, 10 ft crepe myrtle trees for sale offer established specimens that provide instant height and structure, though they require more careful transplanting and cost considerably more than smaller saplings.

Popular Purple Crepe Myrtle Varieties and Color Options

Purple crepe myrtle cultivars rank among the most sought-after varieties for their rich, vibrant blooms that create stunning summer displays. The 'Catawba' variety produces deep purple flowers and grows 10 to 15 feet tall, making it suitable for medium-sized landscapes. 'Twilight' offers dark purple blooms with exceptional mildew resistance and reaches heights of 12 to 15 feet. For true violet-purple coloration, 'Purple Magic' delivers intense color on a compact 8 to 10-foot frame that works well in smaller spaces.

The crepe myrtle Pink Velour variety has gained significant popularity since its introduction for its unique deep rose-pink flowers that appear darker and more saturated than traditional pink varieties. This semi-dwarf cultivar typically reaches 10 to 12 feet in height and 8 to 10 feet in width, making it manageable for suburban landscapes. Pink Velour demonstrates excellent heat tolerance and maintains its color intensity even during peak summer temperatures, unlike some lighter pink varieties that fade in strong sunlight. The foliage emerges burgundy in spring, matures to dark green with burgundy undertones, and transitions to orange-red in fall.

Beyond purple and pink, the color palette extends to pure white varieties like 'Natchez' with its cinnamon-colored exfoliating bark, bright red cultivars such as 'Dynamite' that holds color without fading, and lavender options like 'Muskogee' that can reach 20 to 25 feet. According to research from the U.S. National Arboretum, which developed many modern disease-resistant varieties, selecting colors based on your landscape design and existing plantings ensures cohesive visual impact throughout the blooming season.

Crepe Myrtle Bark Scale Treatment and Management

Crepe myrtle bark scale (Acanthococcus lagerstroemiae) has emerged as a serious pest problem since its first detection in Texas in 2004, spreading to at least 14 states by 2023. This invasive insect appears as white or gray felt-like encrustations on branches and trunks, with female scales producing pink liquid when crushed. Heavy infestations result in black sooty mold growth that covers leaves and branches, reducing photosynthesis and weakening the tree. The scale feeds on plant sap year-round, causing reduced vigor, stunted growth, and diminished flowering.

The best insecticide for crepe myrtle bark scale depends on timing and infestation severity. Systemic soil drenches containing imidacloprid or dinotefuran applied in April or May provide season-long control by moving through the tree's vascular system. These products typically require 60 to 90 days to reach full effectiveness, so early application is critical. For immediate knockdown of active populations, horticultural oil sprays applied during dormancy (January through March) smother overwintering scales effectively. Research from Clemson University demonstrates that dormant oil applications achieving thorough coverage can reduce populations by 85 to 95 percent.

Integrated pest management combines chemical and cultural controls for long-term success. Foliar applications of insect growth regulators like pyriproxyfen disrupt scale reproduction when applied during crawler stage (May and July in most regions). Contact insecticides containing bifenthrin or cyfluthrin work on active crawlers but require precise timing since they only affect the mobile stage. Natural predators including twice-stabbed lady beetles (Chilocorus stigma) and parasitic wasps provide biological control, though they cannot eliminate established infestations alone. Maintaining tree health through proper watering, fertilization, and avoiding excessive nitrogen promotes natural resistance. For detailed management strategies, consult resources from the University of Georgia Extension or the Texas A&M AgriLife Extension, which have published extensive research on this pest.

Planting and Pruning Best Practices

The best time to plant crepe myrtle is during early spring after the last frost date when soil temperatures reach 60°F or higher, typically March through May in most regions. Fall planting works equally well from September through October, allowing roots to establish during cooler weather before winter dormancy. Container-grown specimens can be planted throughout the growing season with proper care, though summer planting requires vigilant watering during establishment. Avoid planting during extreme heat or when soil is frozen, as stress during these periods significantly reduces survival rates.

Crepe myrtle seeds offer an economical propagation method, though seedlings take 3 to 5 years to reach blooming size and may not replicate parent plant characteristics. Seeds develop in brown capsules that mature in late fall and persist through winter. Collect capsules when they turn brown but before they split open, then dry them indoors for 2 to 3 weeks. Extract seeds by crushing capsules and removing chaff. Stratify seeds in moist sand at 40°F for 60 days to improve germination, then sow in well-draining potting mix at 70 to 75°F. Germination typically occurs within 14 to 21 days under consistent moisture and light conditions.

Knowing when to trim crepe myrtle prevents the common practice of 'crepe murder' - severe topping that creates ugly knobs and weakens branch structure. The optimal pruning window is late winter (February through early March) before new growth begins. Remove crossing branches, suckers from the base, and any dead or diseased wood. Prune branches back to a main trunk or lateral branch, never leaving stubs. For mature trees, selective thinning of interior branches improves air circulation and light penetration. Limit removal to no more than 25 to 30 percent of the canopy in any single year. Summer deadheading of spent flower clusters encourages additional bloom cycles, extending the flowering season into fall. The Morton Arboretum provides detailed pruning guides that demonstrate proper techniques for maintaining natural tree form.
